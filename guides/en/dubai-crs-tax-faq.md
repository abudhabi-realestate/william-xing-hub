# Dubai · CRS & Tax Compliance — Frequently Asked Questions

> Compiled from OECD CRS / CRS 2.0, CARF, IPI MCAA public frameworks, and regulations issued by the UAE Federal Tax Authority (FTA).  
> For reference only. This does not constitute tax, legal, or investment advice. Consult licensed professional advisers for your specific situation.

## 1. Understanding CRS: What Is It?

**Q1: What does CRS have to do with me?**

If you remain a tax resident of China and hold financial accounts outside China (including in Dubai)—bank deposits, securities, funds, insurance with cash value, and similar—the relevant account information may be exchanged with the Chinese tax authorities under CRS.

CRS exchanges **account information** (balances, annual interest/dividends/other income, proceeds from asset sales, etc.), not transaction-by-transaction records. Once received, the tax authorities will cross-check whether you have lawfully declared your foreign-source income.

**Q2: Are CRS and FATCA the same thing?**

No. FATCA is the United States’ unilateral reporting regime for US tax residents. CRS is a multilateral exchange mechanism promoted by the OECD, with participation from more than 120 countries and jurisdictions. The US is not a CRS participant; UAE banks report US account holders under FATCA.

**Q3: Will CRS cause my “principal” to be taxed?**

No—this is a common misconception. CRS reports account balances and annual income summaries. Whether tax is due, and how much, depends on how each country’s tax law treats your **income**. The balance itself is not the tax base, but undeclared interest, dividends, transfer gains, and similar items may trigger back taxes.

**Q4: Won’t accounts under CNY 1 million be exchanged?**

That is a misconception. The CNY 1 million (or equivalent) threshold affects **enhanced due diligence** for certain pre-existing accounts only—it does not exempt information from exchange. Once you are identified as a tax resident of a participating jurisdiction, the account is generally reportable regardless of size.

## 2. CRS 2.0: What to Watch From 2026

**Q5: What are the fundamental differences between CRS 2.0 and 1.0?**

Four areas are tightening at once:

1. **Broader scope** — E-money accounts (e.g. PayPal, Wise, above certain balance thresholds), central bank digital currencies, and crypto assets held indirectly through institutions are brought into reporting.
2. **Full reporting for multiple tax residencies** — The “pick one” approach is abolished: if you have multiple tax residencies, information is exchanged with each relevant country. Banks will also ask whether you spend more than 90 days per year in other countries and whether you file tax returns elsewhere.
3. **Finer trust look-through** — Controllers’ specific roles must be reported; “indirect distributions” such as tuition payments, interest-free loans, and debt write-offs are explicitly reportable.
4. **Stronger scrutiny of citizenship/residence-by-investment** — Golden Visas, passport programmes, and similar arrangements may trigger enhanced due diligence (EDD) at banks.

**Q6: When will Dubai implement CRS 2.0?**

According to OECD public timelines, the UAE plans to switch to CRS 2.0 in **2028**; CARF (Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework) exchange is also expected to start in a similar window. The EU, UK, and others began implementation from January 2026; the trend will gradually cover major financial centres.

**Q7: Can I “escape” CRS by moving to Dubai with crypto assets?**

Do not count on it. CARF specifically covers cryptocurrency, stablecoins, tradable NFTs, and similar assets, complementing CRS 2.0. In the UAE, the compliant path is truthful reporting and record-keeping; in mainland China, cryptocurrency trading itself remains subject to local regulation. Converting assets into Bitcoin is not a compliance strategy.

**Q8: Will Dubai property be reported directly under CRS?**

**Real property itself is not within CRS reporting scope.** The following situations remain compliance-relevant:

- Rent or sale proceeds entering bank accounts → account information may be exchanged under CRS;
- China tax residents: foreign rental and transfer gains must still be declared under Chinese tax law;
- The OECD is also promoting **IPI MCAA** (exchange of information on foreign real property); first exchanges are expected around 2029–2030. China is not in the first wave, but the long-term trend warrants attention.

**Common misconception:** “CRS 2.0 will directly report property from 2026” — incorrect. Property information exchange is under IPI MCAA, a separate framework from CRS.

## 3. Tax Residency in Dubai (TRC)

**Q9: What is a UAE Tax Residency Certificate (TRC)?**

A TRC (Tax Residency Certificate) is issued by the UAE Federal Tax Authority (FTA) to certify that an individual or entity met UAE tax residency conditions in a given year. Banks use it for CRS due diligence; some countries require it when applying for treaty benefits.

**Note:** A TRC is **evidence of status**, not a “tax exemption licence,” and does not automatically cancel tax residency obligations in other countries.

**Q10: How long must I live in Dubai to apply for a TRC?**

Under FTA rules, eligibility depends on **actual days physically present in the UAE**:

| Scenario | Days in UAE | Key documents |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | More than 183 days | Passport + entry/exit records |
| 2 | 90–183 days | Above + UAE source of income + fixed place of abode (title deed / EJARI lease / utility bills, etc.) |
| 3 | Fewer than 90 days | Evidence that financial interests and centre of life are in the UAE + fixed place of abode |

**Q11: Does a Dubai Golden Visa automatically make me a UAE tax resident?**

**No.** A Golden Visa addresses **lawful long-term residency**; it does not prove that you **actually live in the UAE**.

- TRC application: still based on actual days of residence; a Golden Visa provides **no special advantage**;
- Bank account opening: holding a long-term visa of 5 years or more (including Golden Visa) often triggers **enhanced due diligence**—banks will ask whether residency was obtained through investment, whether you are also a tax resident elsewhere, and will request proof of income, address, and similar documentation.

**Q12: What does it take to “count” as a Dubai tax resident?**

In practice, both **legal** and **factual** elements must align:

- **Legal:** Meet FTA day-count or centre-of-life tests and obtain a TRC;
- **Factual:** Genuinely live, operate, or work in the UAE, with a fixed home, local banking, and evidence of daily life;
- **Compliance:** If you remain a China tax resident, you must declare worldwide income in China; if you have successfully changed tax residency, you must genuinely sever habitual abode ties with China—not “still living in China while declaring Dubai residency.”

Buying property, occasional visits, or merely moving assets **is not enough** for a compliant tax residency arrangement.

**Q13: Will a TRC stop my offshore accounts from being reported back to China?**

**Not automatically.** CRS exchanges information based on the **tax residency** you declare at account opening and that the bank verifies.

- If you remain a China tax resident → offshore accounts (including Dubai accounts) may still be reported to China;
- If you have become a UAE tax resident and the bank accepts that declaration → the direction of exchange changes, but you must lawfully complete tax residency change, clearance, and related procedures **on the China side**—not merely “open an account in Dubai and declare UAE residency.”

False declarations or inaccurate documentation may create serious legal exposure in both the UAE and China.

## 4. Opening Accounts in Dubai & Bank Compliance

**Q14: Will UAE banks report my account information back to China?**

**Yes, if you are identified as a China tax resident.** The UAE has implemented CRS since 2018, using the **Widest Approach**—banks must identify non-UAE tax residents (except Americans, who are handled under FATCA) and exchange information with their jurisdiction(s) of tax residence.

Both China and the UAE are in the CRS exchange network.

**Q15: What do banks look at when opening an account?**

Common review indicators include:

- Tax residency country(ies) declared on the CRS self-certification form;
- Country of ID issuance, current address, telephone country code;
- Valid UAE residence visa (if claiming UAE tax residency);
- Transfer patterns, linked accounts, historical activity, and similar data.

When multiple jurisdictions appear in the profile, banks may request additional proof; inaccurate information may lead to refusal, restrictions, or reporting.

**Q16: At what balance level do banks scrutinise accounts more closely?**

UAE CRS guidance tiers personal accounts as follows:

- **USD 1 million or below:** Basic electronic record review;
- **Above USD 1 million:** Full enhanced review, including paper files; relationship managers who know you are tax resident elsewhere may have a duty to report proactively.

Individual banks may set lower internal thresholds. Do not assume “small accounts are ignored”—CRS has no de minimis exemption.

**Q17: If I open an account in Dubai with a Chinese passport, will I be treated as a China tax resident?**

A passport is an important indicator but not the sole basis. Banks weigh address, visa, self-declaration, and lifestyle evidence together. A Chinese passport holder who lives in Dubai, holds a TRC, and has their centre of life in the UAE may lawfully declare UAE tax residency—provided **facts and documentation align**, not simply because “the account is in Dubai.”

## 5. Dubai Property & Asset Structuring

**Q18: How does buying property in Dubai relate to CRS / tax compliance?**

Consider several layers:

**The property itself:** Not within CRS financial-account reporting; the title is in your name and does not depend on a broker or app channel.

**CRS-relevant aspects:** Rental income deposited in a Dubai bank account, or sale proceeds credited to an account → those accounts may be exchanged; if you remain a China tax resident, foreign-source income must be declared in China.

**Local tax:** The UAE has no personal income tax and no capital gains tax (for individuals), but property-related fees, registration charges, community charges, and similar costs apply, along with reporting obligations in your country of origin (e.g. China).

**Q19: Is Dubai property a good “channel-risk hedge” allocation?**

From a compliance perspective, property and securities follow different logic: the former is physical ownership with direct control; the latter is account-based rights heavily affected by channels and regulation. Tightening of illegal cross-border securities channels in mainland China in 2026 complements CRS information exchange in the same direction—**this is not a signal that assets will be confiscated, but that compliance costs for paper financial assets are rising.**

This does not mean “buying property avoids tax”; Dubai property still requires lawful source of funds, compliant FX, holding-period tax considerations, and long-term policy risk assessment.

**Q20: Is there a tax difference between Dubai and Abu Dhabi?**

**At the federal level, rules are the same:** no personal income tax, no capital gains tax (for individuals), and CRS and TRC rules apply across the entire UAE.

**Differences lie in assets and markets:** supply cycles, price dynamics, and sector logic differ—those are allocation choices, not tax residency choices. Tax planning should be considered under the federal framework, not on the assumption that the two emirates have separate tax systems.

**Q21: Can holding property through a Dubai company avoid CRS?**

**No.** Corporate accounts are entity accounts; if a company is classified as a **Passive NFE** (passive non-financial entity), controlling persons must be reported through look-through rules. Structures in BVI, Cayman, and similar jurisdictions face the same CRS look-through. Nominee arrangements and multi-layer nesting do not remove reporting obligations and may trigger stricter review and anti-money laundering scrutiny.

## 6. Cross-Border Issues for China Tax Residents

**Q22: If my family stays in China but I live long-term in Dubai, am I still a China tax resident?**

**Very likely yes.** China’s tax residency test is not limited to 183 days—having a **domicile** (household registration, family, main economic interests in China) can establish residency. A spouse and children remaining in China is a significant factor in assessing “domicile”; even if you spend fewer than 183 days in Dubai, you may still be treated as a China tax resident, with offshore account information reported to China.

**Q23: “If I don’t stay in China for 183 days, foreign income has nothing to do with China”—is that correct?**

**For most people with Chinese household registration and family ties in China, this is a dangerous misconception.** The 183-day rule mainly applies to individuals **without a domicile** in China. Chinese citizens with a domicile are generally China tax residents regardless of days spent in China and must declare worldwide income in China.

**Q24: If I already paid tax abroad on foreign income, do I still owe tax in China?**

If you remain a China tax resident, worldwide income is reportable in China; foreign tax paid may be credited **within limits** under the foreign tax credit rules—it is not “paid abroad, so China has no claim.” Keep proof of foreign tax payment and declare according to Chinese tax law.

**Q25: What should I do if I receive a tax notice or discover past under-reporting?**

Act promptly:

1. Verify the notice is genuine (beware of fraud; confirm in person with the competent authority if needed);
2. Organise offshore account statements, income records, and tax payment certificates;
3. Consult professionals familiar with both Chinese and UAE tax law;
4. Voluntary correction within a compliant framework is usually preferable to waiting for an audit.

Since 2025, Chinese tax authorities have stepped up use of CRS data; 2026 guidance emphasises review of 2022–2024 income. A window remains, but delay is risky.

## 7. Compliance Red Lines: What You Must Not Do

**Q26: Which “pseudo-compliance” tactics carry very high risk?**

The following recur in practice and are often clear red lines:

| Practice | Risk |
|----------|------|
| Nominee / friend-held accounts | CRS look-through; possible false reporting, AML exposure |
| Emptying accounts at year-end, refilling in January | Transaction monitoring system (TMS) red flags |
| False CRS self-certification | Potential criminal liability in multiple jurisdictions |
| Split FX purchases (“ant moving”) | Breach of foreign exchange regulations |
| Visa/property only, no actual residence, yet claiming Dubai tax residency | Bank EDD, GAAR (general anti-avoidance) risk |
| Small-country passport + new identity only at bank | Banks use holistic checks; cannot override true tax residency |

**Q27: What does a compliant path look like?**

A four-step framework (for those living in or allocating assets to Dubai):

1. **Confirm tax residency status** — What is your status in China, the UAE, and other relevant countries? Dual residency?
2. **Map global accounts and assets** — Banks, securities, insurance, trusts, property cash flows;
3. **Review filing history** — Truthful declarations in each jurisdiction? Accurate CRS forms? Complete documentation?
4. **Define an action plan** — Correct where needed; change residency through lawful procedures; consult advisers on both China and UAE sides where necessary.

Compliance cost is usually manageable; post-audit back taxes, late payment interest (China: from 0.05% per day), and penalties (tax evasion: 0.5–5× the tax due) are the real uncertainty.

## 8. The Dubai Perspective: How to Read These Rules

**Q28: Is Dubai still worth considering for long-term positioning?**

From a compliance framework standpoint, Dubai/UAE strengths include: **zero personal tax, zero capital gains tax (individuals), no FX controls, and an open environment for living and business**. If you genuinely become a UAE tax resident, CRS exchange direction for your accounts adjusts accordingly.

But advantages are not a “regulatory vacuum”: CRS, AML, bank EDD, and home-country reporting obligations all apply. A TRC’s value is **clarifying status**, not “hiding assets.”

Geopolitical and policy risk always exists; long-term compliance means genuine residence + lawful status + truthful reporting—not short-term “identity packaging.”

**Q29: Should I get a TRC first, or sort out my China-side status first?**

Depends on your facts, but sequencing is usually:

- First confirm where your **actual centre of life** is and whether you have a factual basis to change tax residency;
- Then decide whether to apply for a UAE TRC and how to declare to banks;
- If changing China-side status or clearing tax obligations, complete professional assessment **before** the change—avoid “identity switched, obligations uncleared.”

**Q30: When should I engage professionals?**

Consult advisers (China–UAE bilateral experience preferred) if you:

- Hold accounts or property in multiple countries;
- Retain Chinese household registration but live long-term in Dubai;
- Hold a Golden Visa or are considering a TRC;
- Have used offshore accounts without declaring to China;
- Plan to sell Dubai property or move large sums cross-border;
- Receive inquiries from tax authorities or bank compliance teams.
